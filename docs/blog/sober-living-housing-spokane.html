How to Find Sober Living Housing in Spokane, WA – Complete Guide

Stable housing is one of the most powerful tools in long-term recovery. Here's how to find it.

March 2026  ·  7 min read

For many people in recovery, finding stable housing is the make-or-break moment. It's hard to stay sober when you're sleeping on a couch, bouncing from place to place, or surrounded by people who are still using. Sober living homes offer something that's rare and genuinely powerful: a safe, structured, drug-free environment surrounded by people who understand exactly what you're going through. This guide walks you through everything you need to know about finding sober living housing in Spokane — from what to expect on the first day to how to pay for it if you have no income.

1. What Is Sober Living?

The terms "sober living," "halfway house," and "transitional housing" are often used interchangeably, but they're actually different things. Understanding the distinction helps you find the right fit.

Sober Living Home

A privately run, peer-supported residence. Residents hold jobs, attend meetings, and pay rent. No clinical treatment on site.

Halfway House

Often state-licensed and post-incarceration focused. May include case management, counseling, or court-mandated stays.

Transitional Housing

Time-limited, subsidized housing designed to bridge the gap between homelessness or treatment and permanent housing.

Most sober living homes in Spokane are privately operated and rely on a peer-accountability model — residents hold each other responsible for maintaining sobriety and following house rules. No clinical staff, but often close ties to AA/NA and treatment programs.

2. Who Qualifies for Sober Living in Spokane?

Requirements vary by home, but most Spokane sober living programs look for the following:

  • Commitment to sobriety: Most homes require at least a few days clean before move-in, often confirmed by a drug test.
  • Willingness to follow house rules: Curfews, chore schedules, mandatory meeting attendance, and zero-tolerance substance policies are standard.
  • Ability to contribute financially: Most homes charge weekly or monthly rent, typically ranging from $150–$600/month. Many work with residents who are starting from zero.
  • No violent criminal history: Many homes exclude individuals with recent violent offenses, though policies vary.
  • Active participation in recovery: AA/NA attendance, a sponsor, or involvement in outpatient treatment is often required.
Don't disqualify yourself before you apply. Requirements differ widely between homes. If one doesn't fit, another might.

3. How to Find a Sober Living Home in Spokane

There are several practical ways to locate available sober living options in Spokane:

  1. Get Sober Spokane Shelter & Housing Directory: Browse our curated list of Spokane-area sober living homes, transitional housing, and emergency shelters — updated regularly.   Browse the shelter & housing directory →
  2. Get Sober Spokane Recovery Map: View housing resources on an interactive map by neighborhood.   Open the recovery map →
  3. Ask at your AA/NA meeting: Many sober living home operators are active in the recovery community. Your meeting is often the fastest referral network.
  4. Contact Frontier Behavioral Health: Their case managers maintain up-to-date lists of available sober housing and can make referrals directly.
  5. SAMHSA Behavioral Health Treatment Locator: Search at   findtreatment.gov for licensed facilities and sober living homes.
  6. Washington 211: Call or text 2-1-1 for a live referral to available housing in Spokane County, including sober living options.

4. What to Expect When You Move In

Moving into a sober living home for the first time can feel overwhelming. Here's what a typical first week looks like:

  • Intake and drug test: Expect a urinalysis on arrival and periodically throughout your stay. This protects everyone in the home.
  • House meeting: You'll be introduced to housemates, given the house rules, and assigned chores.
  • Room setup: Most homes provide a furnished bedroom. Bring personal hygiene items, basic clothing, and any medications with prescriptions.
  • Meeting requirements: Most homes require attending at least 3–5 AA/NA meetings per week. Some require a sponsor within 30 days.
  • Community expectations: Quiet hours, guest policies, kitchen cleanliness, and shared responsibilities are taken seriously. Respect and punctuality go a long way.
What to bring
  • Photo ID (driver's license or state ID)
  • Social Security card if applying for benefits
  • Prescription medications in original bottles
  • Basic clothing for a week or two
  • Any court documents or treatment discharge papers
What to leave behind
  • Any alcohol or drugs (zero tolerance)
  • Mouthwashes or products containing alcohol
  • Weapons of any kind
  • Anyone who isn't willing to respect house rules

5. Paying for Sober Living

Cost is often the biggest barrier. Here's the reality: many people enter sober living with nothing — and there are options.

  • State-funded transitional housing: Washington State's Department of Commerce funds several transitional housing programs in Spokane County at no cost to residents. Ask your case manager or call 2-1-1.
  • DSHS coverage: Some sober living homes partner with the Washington Department of Social and Health Services to cover rent for eligible individuals.
  • Oxford House scholarship fund: Oxford House — a national network of self-run recovery homes — maintains a loan program for residents who can't cover the first month's rent.
  • Recovery scholarships: Some faith-based and nonprofit sober living homes in Spokane offer partial or full rent scholarships for people in early recovery with demonstrated need.
  • Work-trade arrangements: A handful of homes allow residents to contribute labor (maintenance, yard work, cooking) in exchange for reduced rent during the first 30–60 days.
  • Employment as a bridge: Many residents start working within their first week. Even a part-time job covers most sober living costs in Spokane.
Don't let cost stop you from reaching out. Most homes would rather work with a motivated person than turn them away.

6. What Happens After Sober Living?

Sober living is a bridge — not a destination. The goal is to build the stability, routines, and support network that make independent sober living possible.

Here's how to set yourself up for a successful transition out:

  • Secure employment first: Most residents begin job searching within the first few weeks. Spokane has strong free employment and skills training programs.   Explore employment resources →
  • Build savings: Even saving $200–$300/month puts you in a strong position to sign a lease or move into your own apartment within 6 months.
  • Stay connected to your support network: Keep going to meetings. Keep calling your sponsor. The people you meet in sober living often become your closest friends.
  • Consider peer recovery coaching: Many past sober living residents give back by becoming peer coaches — a meaningful and paid way to stay connected to the community.
  • Use the resource directory: From food assistance to mental health services, Spokane's recovery network extends well beyond housing.   Browse all resources →
