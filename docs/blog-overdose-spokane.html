What to Do If Someone Is Overdosing in Spokane

Published on: October 30, 2024 • 5 min read

Witnessing a drug or alcohol overdose is a terrifying experience, but knowing exactly how to react can mean the difference between life and death. Spokane County, like many regions, has seen a severe rise in opioid and synthetic fentanyl overdoses over the past few years.

MEDICAL EMERGENCY: CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY

If you suspect someone is overdosing right now in Spokane, do not wait. Call 911 immediately. Tell the dispatcher exactly where you are and that the person is not breathing or is unresponsive.

1. Recognize the Signs of an Overdose

Overdoses look different depending on the substance involved (opioids vs. stimulants vs. alcohol). However, common signs of a life-threatening opioid or fentanyl overdose include:

  • Unresponsiveness: They cannot be woken up, even if you rub your knuckles hard against their sternum (breastbone).
  • Breathing Issues: Breathing is very slow, erratic, shallow, or has stopped entirely. You may hear deep snoring, gurgling, or a "death rattle."
  • Color Changes: Skin, lips, and fingernails turn pale, blue, or gray (depending on skin tone).
  • Pupils: Extremely small, "pinpoint" pupils.
  • Pulse: A slow, erratic, or absent heartbeat.

2. Administer Naloxone (Narcan) Immediately

If you suspect an opioid overdose, administer Naloxone if you have it.

Where to get Narcan in Spokane: Naloxone nasal spray is available without a prescription at almost all Spokane pharmacies (like Walgreens and Rite Aid) under the state's standing order. The Spokane Regional Health District also provides resources and training for obtaining Narcan.

How to Use:

  1. Peel back the package to remove the device.
  2. Place the tip of the nozzle in either nostril until your fingers touch the bottom of the patient's nose.
  3. Press the plunger firmly to release the dose into the patient's nose.
  4. Note: Narcan only reverses opioid overdoses (fentanyl, heroin, oxycodone). It will not harm someone if they are overdosing on a non-opioid, so if in doubt, use it .

3. Call 911 (The Good Samaritan Law)

After administering Narcan, or if you do not have Narcan available, call 911 immediately .

Do not let the fear of legal trouble stop you. Washington State has a Good Samaritan Overdose Law (RCW 69.50.315). This law protects both the person overdosing and the person calling 911 from being charged with drug possession if they seek medical assistance in an overdose situation.

4. Place the Person in the Recovery Position

If you must leave the person for a moment to get help or find Narcan, or if they begin breathing again after Narcan but are still unresponsive, place them in the Recovery Position to prevent them from choking on their own vomit.

  • Roll them onto their side.
  • Bend their top knee forward to stabilize them and prevent them from rolling onto their stomach.
  • Turn their face to the side and rest their head on their top arm.

5. Stay With Them Until Help Arrives

Never leave a person who is overdosing alone unless you absolutely must leave to call 911 or get Narcan. If you administered Narcan, they may wake up agitated, confused, or aggressive as they are immediately thrown into withdrawal. Reassure them that help is on the way.

Important: Narcan wears off in 30-90 minutes, but opioids can last much longer in the system. The person can slip back into an overdose once the Narcan wears off, which is why emergency medical responders must evaluate them.

Long-Term Recovery in Spokane

An overdose is often the catalyst that forces an individual to seek professional help. After the immediate crisis has passed and the person is medically stabilized, the next vital step is comprehensive addiction treatment.

Spokane offers extensive medical detox centers , rehab facilities , and long-term recovery resources to help individuals achieve lasting sobriety.
